# NutriWise Real User Interview Guide

**Purpose:** Validate or invalidate assumptions surfaced during synthetic persona interviews with real users. Target ~45–60 min per session, moderated, ideally with screen-share and the live prototype open.

**Before you start:** Recruit across the three archetypes (dietary restrictor, early-adopter/design-driven, busy/low-effort professional) plus at least 1–2 people who don't fit any archetype cleanly — the synthetic round only tested people who match a profile perfectly.

---

## 1) Questions to Test Synthetic Assumptions

Goal: directly stress-test the five assumptions the synthetic round left unverified.

| # | Assumption being tested | Question |
|---|---|---|
| 1.1 | Receipts are a viable proxy for consumption | "Walk me through your last grocery trip — what did you buy, and what would your receipt actually capture vs. miss?" |
| 1.2 | Habit survives past novelty | "Think of the last app you tried to use daily or weekly. What was true about week one that stopped being true by week three?" |
| 1.3 | Personalization drives activation, not just satisfaction | *(Show onboarding screen)* "If this asked about your diet type right now, would you answer honestly, skip it, or not notice it was missing?" |
| 1.4 | Users trust AI nutrition guidance enough to act | *(Show a real sample recommendation, e.g. "swap chicken for lentils this week")* "Would you actually do this? What would you want to check first?" |
| 1.5 | Stated price tolerance matches real payment behavior | *(Show the pricing screen)* "You're at this exact screen, on your phone, right now — what happens next?" |

---

## 2) Probing Questions for Emotional Context AI Missed

Goal: surface ambivalence, identity tension, and feelings that don't resolve into a clean stance — the thing scripted personas can't produce.

- "What's the *last* time a nutrition or health app made you feel judged, embarrassed, or talked down to? What exactly did it say or show?"
- "Is there a part of you that wants an app to prove something to someone else — a doctor, a partner, people who ask you questions about your diet? Say more."
- "If this app told you something you didn't want to hear, how do you think you'd actually react — not how you *should* react?"
- "What would make you feel like this app was built for you specifically, versus built for someone else and adjusted for you?"
- "Is there anything about using this that you'd feel a little embarrassed for someone to see over your shoulder?"
- *(For anyone with a health concern flagged, e.g. cholesterol, iron)*: "How does it feel to have an app potentially make a call your doctor hasn't fully made yet?"

---

## 3) Behavioral Questions About Actual Usage

Goal: get at revealed behavior instead of self-reported intentions. Ask about specific past instances, not general habits.

- "Tell me about the last three receipts you actually had in your hand — where are they now?" *(tests real receipt-retention behavior)*
- "Open your phone right now — what's an app you downloaded excitedly and haven't opened in over a week? What happened?"
- "Who else buys food in your household, and how much of your week's eating comes from purchases you didn't make yourself?"
- "Think about your actual routine after checkout — bagging groceries, getting to the car, getting the kids settled. Where, realistically, would scanning a receipt fit?"
- "How many health or nutrition apps are currently installed on your phone? When did you last open each one?"
- *(After a mock 2-week diary/prototype trial, if feasible)*: "Show me your upload history. What happened on the days you didn't upload?"

---

## 4) Questions Designed to Reveal Contradictions

Goal: create moments where stated values and actual behavior can diverge in the same conversation — don't resolve the tension for them.

- "You said [X, e.g. 'price isn't an issue' / 'I hate calorie counting' / 'I always check privacy policies'] — can you tell me about a specific recent time that was actually true?"
- "If this app gave you a single score or number for how you're doing, would you want it or hate it? Why might you want it *and* resent needing it?"
- "You said you'd research this before downloading — how long, honestly, do you spend reading permissions or privacy policies before you tap accept?"
- "Imagine the app disagrees with something you believe about your own diet — chicken as protein, veganism being sufficient, needing to count calories. What happens?"
- "If a partner, friend, or doctor recommended this to you, would you use it differently than if you found it yourself? How?"
- "Is there a version of 'success' with this app that would actually make you use it *less*, not more?" *(tests the gap between wanting proof/validation and wanting ongoing engagement)*

---

## Facilitator Notes

- **Don't let coherent answers go unchallenged.** If a participant gives a tidy, structured reason, follow with "tell me about one specific time that was true" — this is exactly where synthetic personas failed.
- **Watch for the performance gap.** People answer research questions in ways that make them sound thoughtful. Weight demonstrated/recalled behavior over stated principle.
- **Let silence sit.** Real ambivalence often shows up as a pause or a "hm, I don't know" — don't rush to fill it with a follow-up question.
- **Recruit for contradiction, not fit.** Include people who don't cleanly match Sara/Lena/Tobias; the riskiest blind spots live outside the three archetypes.
